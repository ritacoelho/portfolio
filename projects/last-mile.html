Last Mile: Reducing Delivery Anxiety

Last-mile delivery is the most expensive, most human and most broken part of the supply chain. This project explored how service design and interaction design could reduce failed deliveries and the anxiety spiral that surrounds them.

Context

Master's project

Year

2022

My role

Lead Researcher + Interaction Designer

Methods

Service safari, co-design, testing

The problem

In Europe, up to 15% of parcels fail on first delivery attempt. Each failed attempt costs the carrier money, adds carbon to the journey and triggers a specific kind of customer anxiety: the parcel is somewhere, you don't know exactly where, and the re-delivery window is vague enough to disrupt your entire day.

The problem isn't just logistical. It's emotional, and existing tracking apps almost never acknowledge that.

Design question: How might we reduce failed deliveries and the anxiety they create, for both customers and couriers, without requiring systemic infrastructure change?

Test

I started with a service safari: ordering several deliveries across different carriers and documenting every touchpoint, from confirmation email to the moment a card landed on my doormat. The experience map revealed three distinct anxiety peaks: at dispatch, when the tracking goes quiet, and during the final "out for delivery" window.

Co-design workshops with eight participants (customers and one courier) surfaced what people actually wanted: not more granular GPS tracking, but more reliable communication and more meaningful control over timing.

My role

I led the research, from planning the service safari and facilitating the co-design workshops through to synthesising findings into a clear design brief. I then led interaction design for the prototype, with particular attention to the moments of highest emotional friction.

Solution

A delivery tracking app built around the three anxiety peaks. Key features: a live route view that shows the courier's progress in honest, legible terms (stops remaining, not an estimated minute count that shifts constantly); a one-tap reschedule available up to 30 minutes before arrival; and a delivery note flow that lets customers leave specific instructions without needing to call.

The visual design deliberately avoided the aggressive urgency of most delivery UIs: no countdown timers, no red alerts. Calm, clear information is more useful than alarming, granular information.

Impact

Usability testing with eight participants achieved 100% task completion across all primary flows. Post-task interviews consistently highlighted the reschedule feature and the route view as moments that "actually made sense", language that signals the design resolved a genuine frustration rather than replicating an existing pattern.
