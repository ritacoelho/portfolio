Pilot: Bridging the Gap Between University and Work

A live industry brief from Atlassian: design an intelligent, AI-powered project management tool for students and new employees — from research to a tested, high-fidelity prototype presented to Atlassian's product and design team.

Client

Atlassian

Year

2023

My role

UX/UI Designer

Team

4 designers

The problem

No project management tool existed for people straddling student and employee contexts at the same time. Atlassian had nothing for new graduates or students in part-time roles — people managing university deadlines, team meetings, and early-career tasks from one place. Students were stitching together Google Drive, Canvas, Discord, and WhatsApp, while professionals used entirely different stacks. The transition between the two was a gap nobody had designed for.

Brief: Design an intelligent project management tool for students and new employees that enhances productivity and team collaboration, sitting within the Atlassian ecosystem.

Test

Research pointed to a consistent underlying problem: 95% of participants wanted one tool for all task types and 86% named task and time management as their biggest pain — not just poor tooling, but the cognitive overhead of context-switching between apps. Competitor analysis confirmed no existing tool bridged student and professional contexts. This shaped three "how might we" questions around personalisation, time allocation, and sustained engagement, which fed directly into the design direction.

Two rounds of Hi-Fi prototyping followed, each iteration responding to specific user feedback before a final presentation to Atlassian's product and design team.

My role

I was one of four UX/UI designers. I led research synthesis, ran ideation (Crazy 8s, decision matrix), and owned two flows end-to-end: the AI project creation model and the notification system. The AI scaffold went through a significant revision between iterations — the first Hi-Fi surfaced all AI-generated tasks at once, which felt overwhelming in testing. The final version staged the reveal: approve milestones first, then see tasks populate. That single change resolved the trust issue users flagged.

Solution

Pilot organises work across academic, workplace, and personal contexts from a single interface. The core insight driving the homepage was that urgency doesn't respect project boundaries — so the app shouldn't either. AI-recommended tasks surface across all active projects in priority order, with a weekly calendar view alongside. A calendar with availability detection suggests meeting times and resolves clashes by offering alternatives rather than just flagging conflicts.

The AI project scaffold lets users paste in an assignment brief and generates a full milestone and task structure. The design challenge was establishing trust in the AI output: users needed to feel in control rather than handed a completed plan. A two-step review — approve milestones first, then tasks populate — gave users the right amount of agency without breaking the flow. A notification system replaces the WhatsApp thread: users can request help from teammates directly in-app, from the project page where the work lives.

All interactions were built inside Atlassian's existing component language so Pilot integrates naturally rather than feeling like a bolt-on. The analytics dashboard (premium) gives teams a per-user view of milestones, workload, and effort — a direct response to the research finding that group accountability was a persistent, unresolved pain point.
